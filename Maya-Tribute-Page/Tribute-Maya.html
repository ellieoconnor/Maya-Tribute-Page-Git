About Maya

Maya Angelou was born Marguerite Annie Johnson on 4 April, 1928 in St. Louis, Missouri. She grew up in St. Louis and Stamps, Arkansas. Angelou was an American poet, memorist, and civil rights activist (amoung many other accomplishments). She published seven autobiographies, three books of essays, several books of poetry, and is credited with a list of plays, movies, and television shows spanning over 50 years. She has received dozens of awards and more than 50 honourary degrees. Angelou is best known for her series of seven autobiographies, which focus on her childhood and early adult experiences. The first, I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings (1969), tells of her life up to the age of 17 and brought her international recognition and acclaim.


In 1959, at the request of Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., Angelou became the Northern coordinator for the Southern Christian Leadership Conference. From 1961 to 1962, she was associate editor of The Arab Observer in Cairo, Egypt. It was the only English-language weekly news in the Middle East. From 1964 to 1966 she was feature editor of the The African Review in Accra, Ghana. She returned to the United States in 1974 and was appointed by Gerald Ford to the Bicentennial Commission and later by Jimmy Carter to the Commission for International Women of the Year. In 1982, Angelou accepted a lifetime appointment as Reynold's Professor of American Studies at Wake Forest University in Winston-Salem, North Carolina. In 1993, she wrote and delivered a poem, On the Pulse of Morning, at the inauguration for President Bill Clinton. In 2000, she received the National Medal of Arts, and in 2010 she was awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom by President Barack Obama.


Maya Angelou was the first black woman director in Hollywood. Angelou wrote, produced, and starred in productions for stage, film, and television. In 1971, she wrote the original screenplay and musical score for the film Georgia, Georgia. Angelou was both author and executiv of a five-part television mini-series Three Way Choice. She also wrote and produced several prize-winning documentaries, including Afro-Americans in the Arts - a PBS special for which received the Golden Eagle Award. Angelou was nominated twice for a Tony Award for acting: once for her Broadway debut in Look Away (1973), and again for her performance in Roots (1977).


On 28 May, 2014, Maya Angelou died in Winston-Salem, North Carolina. She was 86 years-old.

A Timeline of Maya's Life

  • 4 April, 1928 - Maya Angelou is born in St. Louis, Missouri.
  • 1935 - Maya falls silent for 5 years after physical abuse.
  • 1943 - Maya becomes the first African-American female streetcar conductor.
  • 1945 - Gives birth to son, Guy Johnson.
  • 1951 to 1954 - Maya Angelou is married to Tosh Angelos.
  • 1952 - Begins her dancing career as Maya Angelou.
  • 1954 through 1955 - Angelou toured Europe with a production of the opera Porgy and Bess.
  • 1957 - Angelou records her first album, Miss Calypso.
  • 1959 - Angelou moves to New York, where she joined the Harlem Writers Guild.
    • She acted in Jean Genet's off-Broadway production, The Black's, and performed Cabaret for Freedom.
  • 1960 - Meets Martin Luther King Jr., wans was named the Southern Christian Leadership Conferences' (SCLC) Northern Coordinator.
  • 1961 - Angelou moved to Cairo, Egypt.
    • She served as editor of the English-language weekly: The Arab Observer.
  • 1962 - Angelou moved to Ghana, where she taught at the University of Ghana's School of Music and Drama.
    • She worked as a feature editor for The African Review and wrote for The Ghanian Times.
  • 1965 - Returned to the United States to work with Malcolm X in the Organization of African American Unity.
  • 1969 - Published I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings
    • Received the Chubb Fellowship from Yale University.
  • 1972 - Angelou's film Georgia, Georgia came out. Angelou wrote the screenplay and composed the score. Her script was the first ever written by an African American woman to be filmed, and was nominated for a Pulitzer Prize.
  • 1974 to 1983 - Married to Paul du Feu.
  • 1974 - Published Gather Together in My Name.
  • 1976 - Published Singin' and Swingin' and Gettin' Merry Like Christmas.
  • 1977 - Appeared in the television adaption of Alex Haley's Roots.
  • 1981 - Published The Heart of a Woman.
  • 1982 - Joined the faculty at Wake Forest University as a Professor of American Studies.
  • 1986 - Published All God's Children Need Traveling Shoes.
  • 1993 - Angelou recites On the Pulse of Morning at the inauguration for President Bill Clinton.
    • Appeared in John Singleton's Poetic Justice.
    • Won a Grammy for Best Spoken Word or Non-Musical Album for On the Pulse of Morning.
  • 1995 - Won a Grammy Award for Best Spoken Word or Non-Musical Album for Phenomenal Woman.
  • 1996 - Directed her first feature film, Down in the Delta.
  • 2000 - Awarded the National Medal of Arts by President Bill Clinton.
  • 2002 - Published A Song Flung Up to Heaven.
    • Won a Grammy Award for Best Spoken Word or Non-Musical Album for, A Song Flung Up to Heaven.
  • 2008 - Awarded the Lincoln Medal.
  • 2010 - Awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom from President Barack Obama at the White House.
  • 2013 - Published Mom & Me & Mom.
  • 28 May, 2014 - Maya Angelou passed away in her home in Winston-Salem, North Carolina.
